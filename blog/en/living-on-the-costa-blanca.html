Living on the Costa Blanca: climate, healthcare, schools and community

By the Vía Sol team•Updated June 2026•8 min read

Picture an ordinary Tuesday in November. Back in Poland it's already dark by four; here you're on the terrace with a coffee, in short sleeves, looking at the sea. The Costa Blanca enjoys around 320 days of sun a year, mild winters and a Mediterranean rhythm that can lift your mood from one day to the next. It's no accident that more Polish families decide to make the move every year. Here's what life is really like — the beautiful parts, and the practical ones worth keeping in mind.

The climate and your wellbeing

The south of Alicante province has a reputation as one of the healthiest microclimates in the world, an idea made famous by an old study often attributed to the World Health Organization. Labels aside, what you actually feel is concrete: winter temperatures rarely drop below ten degrees, the sea breeze keeps summers from being stifling, and it hardly rains. What does that mean day to day? Life happens outdoors. A walk along the promenade at eight in the evening, padel in January, lunch on the beach in March. Sun and movement stop being a weekend plan and become routine — and over time you really feel it, in your body and your mood.

Healthcare: you're well covered

It's one of the questions we hear most, and the answer is reassuring: Spain has an excellent public health system, with modern hospitals and good doctors. How you access it depends on your situation, so let's take it in turn.

  • Visiting or newly arrived: for trips and short stays you're covered by the European Health Insurance Card (EHIC), which in Poland is the EKUZ (free, issued by the NFZ). It covers medically necessary care in the public system — not private treatment, and not repatriation.
  • If you're a pensioner: you transfer your cover with the S1 form (arranged through the NFZ/ZUS). You register with the INSS, receive your health card and get full access to public healthcare; Poland reimburses Spain. It also covers your dependants.
  • If you work here: employed or self-employed, you pay into the Seguridad Social, and that puts you fully inside the system. You request your health card at the local health centre (centro de salud) with your NIE, your padrón registration and your social security number.
  • If you're living off savings, without an S1: the usual route is a private insurance policy with full cover, no co-payments and no waiting gaps (around €50–150 a month; Sanitas, Adeslas, DKV, Asisa). It also satisfies the health-insurance condition for the residence certificate. Another option, after a year on the padrón, is the convenio especial: about €60 a month if you're under 65 (€157 over that), though it doesn't include the pharmacy subsidy.

An easy rule to remember

EHIC for trips, S1 if you're a pensioner, Seguridad Social if you work, and private insurance or the convenio if you're living off savings. Almost no one ends up uncovered.

Schools and family life

For anyone coming with children, this is usually the decision that weighs most — and there's plenty of choice. Along the coast you'll find international schools with British or International Baccalaureate (IB) curricula, taught in English, where children of many nationalities mix. Spanish state schooling, for its part, is free; to enrol your children you'll need to be registered on the padrón (empadronamiento) in your town. And one thing that always reassures parents: children pick up Spanish at a frankly startling speed. Within a few months they're translating for their parents at the supermarket and making friends in the park without a second thought.

Community and language

Moving here doesn't mean starting from scratch among strangers. On the Costa Blanca, especially in the south — with Torrevieja and Orihuela Costa leading the way — there's a very large Polish and international community. There are Polish shops where you'll find your usual products, churches with Mass in Polish, doctors who speak your language, and groups that organise everything from day trips to help with paperwork. Daily life runs in Spanish, yes, but you rarely feel alone. And for ties back home, direct flights between Alicante and several Polish cities make visiting family a weekend matter, not an expedition.

The cost of living

Here we should be honest, and the news is good: everyday life tends to work out cheaper than in much of Western Europe. Eating out is still affordable — a decent set lunch, a beer with a tapa — the weekly markets fill your basket with fruit and vegetables for very little, and many day-to-day costs sit lighter on your wallet. Not everything is cheaper, of course: some imported goods, or the electricity bill, can surprise you. And in the housing column you'll need to factor in the annual IBI tax and community fees. We break it all down plainly in what buying a home in Spain really costs.

Getting around

Connections are one of the great points in its favour. Alicante–Elche airport is well served and a short hop from the whole coast. The AP-7 motorway runs the length of the province from north to south, linking towns and cities without fuss. And if you'd rather leave the car, the TRAM connects Alicante with Benidorm along the shore — a ride worth taking for the views alone.

Where Poles tend to settle

Broadly, the Costa Blanca splits into two moods. In the north — Benidorm, Calpe, Altea, Jávea — the landscape is greener and more mountainous, with coves, a touch quieter and more genteel. In the south — Torrevieja, Orihuela Costa, Guardamar — the land is flatter, there's even more sun, prices are often gentler, and you'll find the largest Polish community. Neither is better than the other — it depends on the life you're after. You can explore both in more detail in our zones.

In short

  • Around 320 days of sun, mild winters and outdoor life all year round.
  • Excellent public healthcare: EHIC for visits, S1 if you're a pensioner, Seguridad Social if you work, private insurance or the convenio if you live off savings.
  • International schools and free state schooling; children pick up Spanish quickly.
  • A large Polish community in the south, direct flights to Poland and a generally lower cost of living.
