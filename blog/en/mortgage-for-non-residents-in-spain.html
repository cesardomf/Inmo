Mortgage for non-residents in Spain: requirements and terms

By the Vía Sol team•Updated June 2026•8 min read

Let's start with the question that actually keeps people awake: yes, as a non-resident you can get a mortgage in Spain. And as an EU citizen you start with an advantage — your terms are better than a non-EU buyer's. It isn't quite the mortgage a resident would sign, but it's perfectly within reach. Let's go through it with real numbers: how much the bank will lend you, on what terms, and what it will want from you.

How much does the bank lend? It comes down to LTV

LTV (loan to value) is the percentage of the property's value the bank is willing to lend you. Here's the first important difference:

  • Residents: around 80%.
  • Non-residents: usually between 60% and 70%. As an EU citizen with verifiable income you'll typically land nearer 70%; non-EU buyers more often stop at 50-60%.

In plain money terms: you have to put down a 30-40% deposit, and on top of that add roughly 13% in taxes and costs for the purchase. On a property of €200,000, that means having around €60,000-80,000 ready for the deposit and another €26,000 or so for the costs. Worth being clear on before you fall for a particular house.

The 13% in costs is separate from the deposit

The most common mistake is confusing the deposit with the total cost. The mortgage covers part of the price, but the taxes, the notary and the registry are on you. We break it all down in our guide to what buying a home in Spain costs.

The bank lends on the lower figure

This is important and not at all intuitive: that 60-70% isn't worked out on the price you pay, but on the lower of two numbers: the purchase price or the bank's valuation (tasación). The valuation is an independent, official appraisal of the property; it costs between €300 and €500 and you pay for it (it isn't refunded even if you don't end up signing).

And if the valuation comes in below the price? Say you agree to buy for €200,000 but the surveyor values the house at €180,000. The bank applies its 70% to €180,000, that is €126,000, not to €200,000. That €20,000 gap you have to cover yourself, on top of the deposit. So before you settle on a price, it pays to have a realistic idea of the valuation.

Term and age

The usual term for non-residents runs from 20 to 25 years (a resident can reach 30). There's a cap that catches a lot of people out: most banks require you to finish paying before you turn 75 (some, 70). If you apply at 60, don't expect a 25-year term — you'll be offered 10 or 15. The applicant's age shortens the term, and a shorter term pushes up the monthly payment.

Interest rates in 2026

Today you're choosing between two options:

  • Fixed rate: roughly between 3.5% and 4.8% for non-residents. The payment never changes over the life of the loan — peace of mind in exchange for a slightly higher starting point.
  • Variable rate: 12-month Euribor plus a margin of around 0.7%-1.2%. The payment rises or falls with Euribor.

I'm deliberately not giving you an exact Euribor figure here: it moves every day. Before you do the maths, check the live Euribor (the Bank of Spain, Banco de España, publishes it) rather than trusting a number you read months ago. And a current note: in early 2026 several banks pulled fixed rates on non-resident loans above roughly €500,000, so on larger deals you may only find variable on offer.

How much can you borrow? Debt-to-income

Spain doesn't grant the mortgage on the value of the house alone — it looks at your ability to pay. The rule banks use is debt-to-income: the sum of all your monthly payments (the new mortgage plus any other loan you have) shouldn't exceed 30-35% of your net monthly income. If between the car, another mortgage and credit cards you already pay a lot each month, that reduces what the bank will let you borrow, even when the valuation leaves plenty of room.

What documents the bank asks a non-resident for

This is the part worth preparing calmly and early. For an EU buyer the paperwork is simpler than for a non-EU one, but the bank will want to see, at a minimum:

  • Your passport or national ID and your NIE (essential).
  • Your last 3 to 6 payslips.
  • Your tax returns for the last 2 years (your Polish PIT).
  • Your employment contract (or company accounts if you're self-employed).
  • Bank statements for the last 6 to 12 months.
  • A credit report from your country (your record of debts).
  • Proof of the deposit funds: where the money you'll put in comes from.

Most of it will be asked for with a sworn translation into Spanish. It isn't complicated, but it takes time: the sooner you gather it, the sooner you'll get an answer.

Since 2019, the bank pays the mortgage-deed costs

Good news: under the 2019 mortgage law (LCCI), the bank covers the tax, the notary, the registry and the gestoría for the mortgage deed. In practice your only out-of-pocket cost for the mortgage itself is the valuation. That said, with a mortgage a home insurance policy is required (at least buildings cover, for the structure), but the law lets you choose your own insurer — the bank can't force you onto its own policy.

The best advice: the mortgage before the house-hunting

If you're going to finance, don't leave the bank until last. Get a mortgage decision in principle (a pre-approval) before you start viewing homes. Knowing exactly how much the bank will lend changes everything: you search in your real range, you negotiate with the weight of someone whose financing is half-sorted, and you don't lose a reservation to a late "no" from the bank. Coming to the seller with your mortgage on track puts you ahead of other buyers.

In short

  • As a non-resident EU buyer you can finance, usually at 60-70% (nearer 70% as an EU citizen).
  • Plan a 30-40% deposit plus 13% in costs; the mortgage is worked out on the lower of price or valuation.
  • Term of 20-25 years, with an age cap around 75; fixed at 3.5-4.8% or variable (Euribor + 0.7-1.2%) — confirm the live Euribor.
  • The bank checks your debt-to-income (payments ≤ 30-35% of net income) and asks for payslips, tax returns and a sworn translation.
  • Since 2019 the bank pays the mortgage deed; you only pay the valuation. Get the approval before you go house-hunting.
