How to buy a property on the Costa Blanca, step by step

By the Vía Sol team•Updated June 2026•9 min read

Buying a home in another country sounds harder than it is, mostly because nobody has told you the order things happen in. And that's the trick: in Spain the process is fairly predictable. A reservation, an arras deposit, the checks, signing at the notary, and not much more. Here it is laid out, from the first call to the keys, with the real timelines and the figures that actually matter.

Step 1 — Preparation: what to have in place before you search

The purchases that run smoothly are the ones that arrive prepared. Before you fall for a particular property, sort out four things:

  • Your NIE, the foreigner's identity number. You can't sign without it, so the sooner you have it, the better. We explain it in the NIE guide.
  • A Spanish bank account. You'll need it for the deposit, the notary, the taxes and, later, the utilities. You can open one as a non-resident.
  • Your real budget: not just the price, but the price plus roughly 13% in taxes and costs. We break that figure down in what buying a home in Spain really costs.
  • If you're financing, a mortgage decision in principle. Knowing what the bank will lend before you start looking saves a lot of disappointment; our mortgage guide for non-residents covers it.

With that done, you come to the table as a serious buyer who can genuinely close. It shows, and the seller appreciates it.

Step 2 — The search and the inspection trip

The real work starts with ruling things out. Tell us what you're after — area, budget, ground floor or with a view, near the sea or the golf course — and we'll put together a shortlist of properties that genuinely fit, without wasting your time on the ones that don't.

Then comes the moment to fly over. The usual format is a two- or three-day inspection trip along the coast: we collect you from Alicante airport and line up the viewings one after another, so that in a single morning you see five or six homes and in the afternoon compare them with a clear head. Seeing is deciding. Almost nobody buys a home they haven't walked through, and rightly so.

Step 3 — The reservation: taking it off the market

When a property convinces you, the first step to secure it is the reservation contract (contrato de reserva). You sign a simple document and pay a holding deposit of around €3,000 to €6,000, which is deducted from the final price. In return, the property comes off the market for an agreed period — usually two or three weeks — while the next documents are prepared.

It's a light but real commitment: it locks the home so nobody snatches it from under you while your lawyer runs the checks. This is the moment to commit with your head on, not to sign blind.

Step 4 — The arras contract

The next rung is the arras contract (contrato de arras), the real private sale agreement. Here you typically hand over 10% of the price (the reservation deposit counts towards it) and the price, deadlines and a cut-off date for signing at the notary are all fixed.

The crucial detail is the type of arras. The Spanish standard is arras penitenciales, governed by Article 1454 of the Civil Code (Código Civil), which work like a mutual insurance: if the buyer pulls out, they lose the deposit; if the seller is the one who backs out, they pay double. That's why the contract should expressly invoke that article — it protects you as much as it binds you.

Arras, in one line

You walk away and lose your deposit; the seller walks away and pays you double. Balanced, clear and in writing.

Step 5 — Due diligence: check before you pay

Between the arras and the signing, the checks are done — and this is where a good lawyer is worth every euro. The centrepiece is the nota simple from the Land Registry (Registro de la Propiedad): an official document that states who the real owner is and what charges sit on the property — mortgages, embargoes, easements. Anything that doesn't come back clean here doesn't get signed.

Alongside the nota simple, it's verified that the community fees are up to date, that the IBI property tax is paid, and that the licences are in order. This is why we keep stressing it: you need an independent lawyer, not the seller's and not the developer's. Their only job is to defend your interests and warn you if something doesn't add up before it's too late.

Step 6 — Completion: deed, taxes and registration

The big day is signing the public deed (escritura pública) before a notary. There you pay the rest of the price (usually by banker's cheque), the notary certifies the transaction and hands you the keys. For that moment the NIE is essential: without a NIE the notary cannot sign, so it has to be ready well in advance.

The taxes follow right after. On a resale you pay ITP transfer tax, which in the Valencia region you have 30 business days to settle (Modelo 600). Once that's done, the notary sends the electronic copy to the Land Registry and the property is registered in your name. From there, it's yours in every sense.

Step 7 — After completion: getting the home ready

With the keys in hand, a few final formalities remain — the ones that turn a property into a home:

  • Utilities: the change of holder (cambio de titularidad) of electricity and water into your name, with your NIE, your IBAN and the deed. It's quick and, for electricity, legally free.
  • Home insurance: strongly advised always, and mandatory if you have a mortgage. Water damage is the most common claim, so don't leave it for later.
  • Empadronamiento: registering at the town hall (Ayuntamiento). It's free and opens the door to the public health system, school places for the children and local discounts.

Buying off-plan (obra nueva) is a little different

If instead of a finished home you buy off-plan, brand-new property, the path changes a little. Rather than a single payment, there are stage payments during construction: a reservation, a percentage when you sign the private contract, and instalments tied to building milestones, with the bulk at the end.

The key thing to insist on: the developer has a legal obligation to guarantee 100% of the money you advance, plus interest, through a bank guarantee (aval bancario) or surety insurance (seguro de caución) in a segregated account. This is imposed by the Building Act, Ley 38/1999 (LOE), amended by Ley 20/2015. If the build isn't delivered, you get your money back with interest. Don't sign until you've seen those guarantees.

On tax, a new build pays 10% IVA plus AJD, not ITP. To move in and connect the definitive utilities you need the Licencia de Primera Ocupación (first-occupation licence), which certifies that what was built matches the project and is habitable. Before the keys, there's a walk-through inspection and you draw up a snagging list (lista de repasos). And then the LOE warranties protect you: 1 year for finishes, 3 years for habitability (damp, installations) and 10 years for structural defects.

How long does all this take?

For a resale, from signing the arras to picking up the keys usually takes six to ten weeks: the time to run the checks, close the mortgage if there is one, and line up the notary's diary. A new build has no fixed clock: it follows the pace of construction, which can run from a few months to a couple of years depending on the stage you come in at.

In short

  • Prepare the NIE, a Spanish bank account, your budget (price + ~13%) and, if financing, the mortgage in principle first.
  • A reservation (~€3,000–6,000) takes it off the market; then a 10% arras under Art. 1454.
  • The nota simple from the Registry and an independent lawyer check ownership, charges and licences before you pay.
  • Sign at the notary with the NIE ready, ITP within 30 business days, and registration in your name.
  • Resale: ~6–10 weeks from arras to keys; new build follows construction.
