What buying a home in Spain really costs (taxes and fees, 2026)

By the Vía Sol team•Updated June 2026•8 min read

The asking price is not what you'll actually pay. And for many buyers that's the real worry: not the price itself, but the costs you can't see until you're already deep in the deal. We'll spell it out. The quick rule, so you have it from the first minute: budget 11-13% on top of the price for a cash purchase, and up to 13-15% if you take a mortgage. Now to the detail, line by line.

The big tax: it depends on resale versus new build

This is the first and most important split. A resale home and a new build are taxed in completely different ways, and that changes the final bill quite a bit.

Resale: the ITP

If you buy a resale home in the Comunidad Valenciana, you pay Transfer Tax (ITP), currently 10%. The buyer pays it, on form Modelo 600, within 30 working days of signing. On a €250,000 flat that's €25,000 in one go, so it pays to keep it front of mind.

An important change is on the way: from 1 June 2026 the general rate drops to 9% (and rises to 11% on the portion above €1,000,000). There are reduced rates of 8, 6 and 3%, but they require fiscal residency and a habitual home with income limits, so in practice they almost never reach a non-resident buyer.

The tax is calculated on the higher of two values

The tax office doesn't simply take the price on the deed. There's the Land Registry's reference value (valor de referencia), which acts as a minimum tax base: ITP applies to the declared price or the reference value, whichever is higher. If you pay below that value, you'll still be taxed on the cadastral figure. It's a very common surprise, so always check the reference value before you settle on a price.

New build: VAT plus AJD

If you buy a new home directly from the developer, there's no ITP. You pay VAT at 10% and, on top, Stamp Duty (AJD) at 1.5% (dropping to 1.4% from June 2026). That's roughly 11.5% in taxes altogether. The 21% VAT rate applies only to plots or commercial premises, not to your home.

The other costs of buying

On top of the main tax there's a handful of fixed costs. None is huge on its own, but they add up:

  • Notary: between €600 and €875 for the deed, depending on price and length.
  • Land Registry (Registro de la Propiedad): between €400 and €650 to register the home in your name.
  • Independent lawyer: around 1% of the price plus VAT. It's optional, but we always recommend it: your lawyer checks the nota simple, the charges and the contract before you sign anything.
  • Gestoría (admin agency): between €200 and €400 to process taxes and registrations.
  • Valuation (tasación): between €300 and €500, only if you take a mortgage. The buyer pays it and it isn't refundable.

Good news if you finance: the bank pays its share

Since the 2019 mortgage law, the costs of the mortgage deed (its AJD, notary, registry and gestoría) are paid by the bank, not you. Out of the whole mortgage package, the buyer is basically left with the valuation. It's a lot less than people fear.

Plusvalía municipal: watch out if the seller is non-resident

Plusvalía municipal is a local tax on the increase in land value, and as a rule the seller pays it. But there's one exception worth knowing: if the seller is non-resident in Spain, liability shifts to the buyer. It's easy to overlook, and a good lawyer settles it before signing.

What you pay every year as an owner

Buying is a one-off; owning has a yearly cost. It's worth putting in the budget from the start:

  • IBI (property tax): between 0.4% and 1.1% of the cadastral value per year, depending on the town. In Alicante province it's collected through SUMA. For example, Torrevieja is around 0.40% and Guardamar around 0.95%; on the same cadastral value, the gap between one town and another is noticeable.
  • Refuse tax (tasa de basura): since 2025 it's billed separately from the IBI in most councils. It usually runs between €100 and €200 a year.
  • IRNR, Modelo 210 (imputed income): this one catches many people out. If you're non-resident and don't rent the home out, you still have to file an imputed income each year. The base is 1.1% or 2% of the cadastral value, and the rate is 19% for EU residents. The amount is modest, but the form still has to be filed.
  • Community fees: from €30 to €100 or more a month for flats; more on resort developments with a pool, gardens or security.
  • Home insurance: between €200 and €400 a year for an average home; a bit more for a villa with a pool.

In short

  • Budget 11-13% on top of the price in cash, and up to 13-15% with a mortgage.
  • Resale: ITP at 10% (9% from June 2026), on the price or the reference value, whichever is higher.
  • New build: 10% VAT plus 1.5% AJD, around 11.5%.
  • Add notary, registry, lawyer and gestoría; with a mortgage, the bank pays its deed and you're left with basically the valuation.
  • Then, every year: IBI, refuse tax, IRNR (Modelo 210), community fees and insurance.
